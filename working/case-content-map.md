## Major Evidence Clusters

Publications and written scholarship: *Eating Smoke*, *Handbook of Digital Public History*, articles and chapters on digital public history, curation, oral history, public memory, materiality, monuments, and urban/environmental history.

Citation and digital scholarship framing: Tebeau's conventional citation record falls within the expected range for a full professor in history. But conventional metrics substantially understate his influence, because much of his scholarship has taken the form of public-facing digital infrastructure. Curatescape and JOTPY show impact at the level of field practice: they have been cited, adopted, adapted, taught with, written about, and used by scholars, students, cultural institutions, and publics. The argument should not be defensive. The numbers are sufficient, and the fuller record is exceptional.

Promotion pathway framing: Pathway 3 or Pathway 4 is the natural fit. The case should argue that Mark combines a strong publication record with an exceptional body of public, digital, and oral history scholarship. Curatescape and JOTPY should be evaluated as major scholarly contributions in embodied form: sustained, field-shaping infrastructures that advance historical arguments, build methods, generate scholarship, and demonstrate impact through use, citation, collaboration, teaching, and public uptake. The language of "monograph-scale" and "equivalent in scope, rigor, and impact" is important because it speaks directly to the promotion criteria, but it should not become a one-to-one conversion argument in which a skeptical reader is invited to decide whether a project equals a book or an article. The better argument is cumulative: the total body of scholarship exceeds the standard.

Equivalency should be argued through the criteria's own terms:

Scope: international adoption, long-term sustainability, hundreds of curators and contributors, multiple institutional partners, and significant scholarly output generated by Curatescape, JOTPY, and related project ecosystems.

Rigor: sustainable codebases and archival infrastructures; explicit methods for curation, metadata, oral history, place-based interpretation, participation, and shared authority; inclusion in scholarly literature; peer-reviewed writing that theorizes the work; and external/post-project reviewers able to assess the scholarly quality of embodied digital/public history.

Impact: scholarly engagement, public interest, press coverage, project adoption, teaching use, partner uptake, citation, public-facing collections, and validation by reviewers who can evaluate both conventional scholarship and project-based scholarly infrastructure.

Digital/public project scholarship: Curatescape, Cleveland Historical, JOTPY, Salt River Stories, Cleveland Regional Oral History Collection / Cleveland Voices, and Mark's founding work at the Center for Public History + Digital Humanities. Treat later CPHDH projects as source leads only unless Mark's direct role is verified.

CROHC / Cleveland Voices impact: Mark created the Cleveland Regional Oral History Collection initiative in 2002 and co-led it through Summer 2014. During that period, he was involved in collecting approximately 1,000 interviews, with the working range currently estimated as at least 944 and fewer than 1,049. The full collection now includes 1,381 interviews, and the EngagedScholarship readership map shows more than 250,350 downloads over two decades.

Oral history standards: OHA and AHA standards support evaluating oral history projects and curated collections as scholarship when they create, preserve, interpret, and diffuse historical knowledge. This is the frame for CROHC / Cleveland Voices: it is an oral history collection of unusual scale and impact, not merely a source base for later scholarship.

Grants and funded leadership: Department of Education Teaching American History grants at Cleveland State, including Academy of American History and Sounds of American History; NEH Mobile Museum Initiative / Mobile Historical; NSF collaboration; Curating Kisumu/MaCleKi; SocialScribe/Oral History Toolkit; Glen Canyon Dam administrative history; Arizona Humanities and partner-supported work.

Grants comparison: the grants record should be compared across the broader academic funding universe, with attention to breadth of support, amount of funding, range of sponsors, collaborative scale, and sustained activity over time. The strongest case is not total dollars alone; it is the conversion of funding into public knowledge infrastructure.

Teaching and mentoring: Public History Methodology, History in the Wild, Memory and Monuments, practicum courses, field-based projects, student public history work, graduate mentoring, and student outputs tied to public-facing projects.

International and national standing: C2DH Luxembourg residency, International Federation for Public History, international talks/workshops, *Handbook of Digital Public History*, project adoption, reviewing/editing work, and external invitations.

Public impact: platform adoption, page views/analytics where available, oral history scale, archive contributors/users, press, citations, partner letters, student outputs, public audiences, and long-term project afterlives.

Models and guidance: strong promotion statements if available, ASU/College/SHPRS guidance, professional association guidance, and peer-institution examples of evaluating public/digital scholarship.

Public evidence showcase: reviewer-facing GitHub pages that present evidence cleanly without overloading the formal file.

Public/private separation: `working/` is private construction; `evidence/` holds source materials; `public-dossier/` is curated and reviewer-facing.

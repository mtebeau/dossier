## Content

The grants case should do more than list awards. It should show that Mark Tebeau repeatedly built fundable intellectual projects across public history, digital humanities, oral history, teacher professional development, environmental history, public archives, community memory, and interdisciplinary research. The record stands well in the broader universe of academic grant funding because of its breadth of support, amount of funding, range of sponsors, collaborative reach, and activity over a sustained period of time.

The central claim:

`Tebeau's grant record demonstrates sustained capacity to turn historical scholarship into fundable public knowledge infrastructure: archives, platforms, oral history systems, teacher institutes, digital tools, public interpretation, metadata frameworks, and interdisciplinary collaborations.`

The strongest version of the grants argument includes funded support, large collaborative projects, and sustained proposal development. Funded grants demonstrate external validation, leadership, and institutional capacity. Proposal activity demonstrates that Mark has repeatedly designed nationally legible projects that could attract serious institutional, federal, foundation, and public-humanities review.

## Draft Case Language

Tebeau's grants record demonstrates a sustained ability to design public-facing humanities projects that funders recognize as consequential. His funded work ranges from public humanities grants to digital oral history infrastructure, teacher professional-development grants, and large interdisciplinary collaborations. His proposal record also shows repeated national-scale efforts to build digital archives, metadata systems, oral history tools, and public history collaborations through NEH, NSF, NEA, Arizona Humanities, Department of Education, and foundation-supported programs.

The record stands out for breadth and duration. Across more than two decades, Tebeau has secured or helped build support from federal agencies, state humanities councils, foundations, universities, and public partners. That support has not clustered around a single narrow project. It has funded teacher professional development, oral history, digital infrastructure, community archives, environmental and urban history, public interpretation, pandemic memory, metadata, and collaborative public knowledge.

The CSU Teaching American History grants are particularly important. They show that before ASU, Tebeau was already the architect and PI of major federally funded history-education collaborations that produced curricula, teacher training, oral histories, public archives, and regional public history infrastructure. Those grants help explain the later emergence of Cleveland Voices, Curatescape, Cleveland Historical, and the broader public/digital history architecture of his career.

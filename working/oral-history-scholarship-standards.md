# Oral History As Scholarship Standards

Working note created 2026-06-17.

## Content

The Cleveland Regional Oral History Collection / Cleveland Voices should be evaluated within professional standards that recognize oral history as scholarship, not merely as source gathering, service, or outreach. The strongest case is that CROHC combines research, interpretation, archival creation, community collaboration, public access, teaching, and long-term scholarly/public use.

Given these definitions, CROHC stands as an oral history collection of unusual scale and impact: 1,381 interviews in the full collection; roughly 1,000 interviews connected to Mark's 2002-Summer 2014 leadership period; and more than 250,350 downloads over two decades.

## Source Standards

Oral History Association, "Guidelines for the Evaluation of Oral Historians":

https://oralhistory.org/oha-guidelines-for-the-evaluation-of-oral-historians/

The OHA guidelines state that oral history involves a recorded, in-depth interview based on substantial preliminary research, preserved, made accessible, and interpreted. They emphasize that oral historians produce traditional and non-traditional outputs, including archival materials, exhibitions, digital history projects, collaborative community work, publicly oriented products, and curated collections.

Most important for CROHC: the OHA guidelines say oral historians conduct research that creates archived primary sources and that oral history research remains valid when scholars produce interview collections for others to use and interpret. This maps directly onto CROHC as a scholarly collection, not merely a repository.

American Historical Association, "Guidelines for Broadening the Definition of Historical Scholarship":

https://www.historians.org/resource/guidelines-for-broadening-the-definition-of-historical-scholarship/

The AHA guidelines explicitly include oral history projects among forms of historical scholarship that may be evaluated through expanded genre and format. They also argue that historical scholarship should be assessed through comparable criteria of originality, quality, and significance, regardless of format.

The AHA frames scholarship through the Smithsonian Institution's broader mission of "the increase and diffusion of knowledge." That formulation is especially useful for CROHC because the project did both: it increased historical knowledge through recorded interviews and diffused that knowledge through a durable public archive and Cleveland Voices.

American Historical Association, "Committee Issues Report on Redefining Historical Scholarship" (1994):

https://www.historians.org/perspectives-article/committee-issues-report-on-redefining-historical-scholarship/

The 1994 AHA report already argued against equating historical scholarship only with the monograph. It described historical scholarship as uncovering and exchanging new information, shaping interpretations, and communicating historical knowledge through books, articles, exhibits, films, historic sites and structures, classrooms, and public venues. This establishes a longer professional genealogy for treating oral history and public historical production as scholarship.

## Dossier Claim

`Professional standards from the Oral History Association and the American Historical Association support evaluating CROHC / Cleveland Voices as scholarship. The project created, preserved, interpreted, and made accessible a large body of primary-source oral history evidence. ## Agent Notes

Do not present this as a defensive exception. The standards establish that oral history collections can be scholarly outputs when they meet standards of research, interpretation, preservation, accessibility, and impact. CROHC's scale and downloads make the claim unusually strong.

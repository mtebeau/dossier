# JOTPY Assessment Starting Point

Working note created 2026-06-17.

This note records the starting assessment developed in conversation with Mark about A Journal of the Plague Year: An Archive of COVID-19. It is not final prose.

## Content

A Journal of the Plague Year should be evaluated as rapid-response archival scholarship, digital public history infrastructure, and a distributed curatorial consortium. Its significance is not only that it collected pandemic materials at scale, but that it modeled a collaborative, participatory, public-facing way to document a global crisis in real time.

The project should be framed as both archive and method: a born-digital archive, a public humanities infrastructure, a pedagogical platform, a curatorial network, and a scholarly argument about metadata, silences, representation, community collecting, and the ethics of rapid-response archiving.

JOTPY may be the world's largest born-digital archive of the COVID-19 pandemic. This is a potentially powerful claim, but it should be tested before use in final dossier prose.

The safer current formulation is:

`JOTPY is among the largest and most visible born-digital rapid-response archives of the COVID-19 pandemic, with more than 20,000 digital objects and a distributed international curatorial consortium.`

JOTPY's public impact is visible not only in press coverage but in its distributed partner ecology: universities, archives, libraries, museums, community organizations, and international collaborators used the project as shared infrastructure for collecting, teaching, curating, and interpreting pandemic experience.

The equivalency argument should be made through scope, rigor, and impact rather than a one-to-one conversion of project to book or article.

Scope: JOTPY operated at the scale of a major born-digital public archive, with more than 20,000 digital objects, several hundred curators, dozens of participating institutions, scholarly and public collaborators, a distributed curatorial consortium, public contributors, students, educators, archivists, and international collaborators.

Rigor: JOTPY's rigor appears in its archival and curatorial methods, metadata practices, attention to silences and representation, rapid-response and rolling-response models, teaching and internship structures, and the direct scholarly literature that theorizes the project.

Impact: JOTPY's impact is visible through scholarly engagement, public interest, press coverage, partner and node adoption, teaching use, citation, professional attention, and validation by reviewers who can evaluate rapid-response archival scholarship and public digital history. The investment of time by several hundred curators from dozens of institutions is itself evidence of the project's intellectual force: scholars, archivists, teachers, students, and public partners chose to devote limited professional time to making the project successful because they recognized the importance of the vision.

## Process And Method

The JOTPY evidence table should include at least four categories:

1. Popular / news media.
2. Professional / scholarly public venues.
3. Partner / node pages.
4. Scholarly / pedagogical mentions.

Build separate tables for:

- press / media coverage;
- partner and node pages;
- scholarly and professional publications;
- pedagogical use;
- project scale and collection metrics;
- international collaborators and collections;
- oral histories and metadata;
- awards, grants, and institutional recognition.

To make the "world's largest" version safe, build a comparison memo that defines:

- born-digital;
- archive;
- pandemic archive;
- largest by number of objects, contributors, partners, collections, or public reach;
- comparable COVID-19 archives and their scale.

## Questions And Issues

Do not reduce JOTPY to a big archive. Its scale matters, but the better case is scale plus method plus participation plus public reach plus scholarly argument.

Do not use the "world's largest" claim until tested. We can be ambitious and conservative at the same time.

Questions to resolve:

- Can we document the more-than-20,000-digital-objects claim from the project itself or ASU source material?
- What comparable COVID-19 archives exist, and how do they report scale?
- How many 2020-2025 press, professional, partner, and pedagogical mentions can be confirmed?
- Which partner pages best demonstrate the distributed ecology of the project?
- Which external reviewers can evaluate rapid-response archival scholarship and participatory public history?

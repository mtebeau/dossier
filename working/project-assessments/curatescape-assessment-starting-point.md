# Curatescape Assessment Starting Point

Working note created 2026-06-17.

This note records the starting assessment developed in conversation with Mark about Curatescape's role in the promotion case. It is not final prose.

## Content

Curatescape should be evaluated as scholarly infrastructure: a software platform and curatorial framework through which Mark Tebeau advanced a method for place-based digital public history. Its significance lies not simply in use or adoption numbers, but in the fact that other scholars, curators, students, and cultural organizations built public history work on infrastructure that Mark conceived and authored.

The case should be direct: Curatescape is widely recognized as an important scholarly contribution in digital public history, even though its primary form is software, platform architecture, and curatorial method rather than a conventional article or monograph.

Curatescape makes a theoretical argument in embodied form. It operationalizes claims about place, landscape, memory, mobility, curation, public interpretation, and shared authority. It should not be described as a technical service product that merely supports scholarship elsewhere. It is scholarship because it embodies method, enables historical interpretation, is taken up by other scholars and curators, and changes practice in the field.

Curatescape demonstrates field-level impact because scholars and curators in the United States and internationally built public history projects on a platform and method Mark authored.

The equivalency argument should be made through scope, rigor, and impact rather than a one-to-one conversion of project to book or article.

Scope: Curatescape has been active since 2010, an unusually long life for a digital humanities project. Its scope includes international adoption, hundreds of curators and contributors, many institutional partners, and substantial scholarly and public output generated by Curatescape-based projects.

Rigor: Curatescape's rigor appears in the sustainability of the codebase and platform model; its explicit methods for place-based interpretation, mobile curation, public history writing, media documentation, and shared authority; its presence in scholarly literature; and the possibility of external/post-project review by scholars able to assess digital public history as scholarship.

Impact: Curatescape's impact is visible through scholarly engagement, public interest, press coverage, platform adoption, teaching use, project afterlives, citation, and the work of scholars and curators who built their own digital public history on infrastructure Mark authored.

The dossier claim should be:

`Curatescape is an authored scholarly infrastructure whose uptake, reuse, citation, and project afterlives demonstrate influence in digital public history, museum studies, mobile interpretation, place-based curation, oral history, and cultural heritage.`

Possible prose after evidence cleanup:

`In a field where citation metrics only partially capture influence, Curatescape's citation record is one measurable indication of broader scholarly uptake, alongside adoption, reuse, project building, teaching, grants, and international use.`

## Process And Method

Do not inflate the citation record.

Use raw count, deduped count, and substantive-count separately:

- Raw count: all imported Zotero items in the Curatescape Google Scholar collection.
- Deduped count: unique works after title/author/year cleanup.
- Substantive count: works that meaningfully cite, discuss, use, teach, or build from Curatescape or Curatescape-related method.

The dossier should use the strongest claim the evidence can bear. If the cleaned count is near or above one hundred unique works, that is a major impact marker in history and public history. If the substantive count is lower, use the lower number and make it work.

Possible wording after cleanup:

`A Google Scholar/Zotero capture identified approximately X references to Curatescape-related work; after deduplication and review, Y unique works demonstrate uptake across digital public history, museum studies, mobile interpretation, place-based curation, oral history, and cultural heritage.`

For each Zotero item, classify:

- direct discussion of Curatescape;
- project built on Curatescape;
- citation to Mark's theory or method;
- adjacent mobile/public history citation;
- teaching/pedagogical use;
- international/non-U.S. uptake;
- noise/remove.
